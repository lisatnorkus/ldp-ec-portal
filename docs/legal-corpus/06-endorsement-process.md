# Endorsement Process

The LDP endorsement process applies **only to nonpartisan races** in Louisville: Mayor and Metro Council. Partisan races have no endorsement step — the Democratic Primary winner is the Democratic nominee, and no party body can endorse one Democrat over another in a Democratic Primary per `[KDP Art. I.I.a]`.

This file documents what's in the bylaws (very little) and what's in the LDP Endorsement Process Committee policy (most of it). The Endorsement Process Committee is an **ad hoc** committee per JCDEC practice as of April 2026 — not a standing committee under `[LJCDP §26]`.

## Why endorsement exists at all (and only for these races)

`[KDP Art. I.I.a]`:

> "No Democrat Committee governed by these By-Laws, or any Democratic Party Officer acting in their official capacity, shall endorse or support one Democratic candidate over another Democratic candidate in a Democratic Primary Election."

This is a flat prohibition for partisan-primary endorsements. The narrow exception `[KDP Art. I.I.b.i]` allows the SCEC to endorse an incumbent statewide KY Constitutional Officer by 3/4 majority + secret ballot — that's a statewide-only mechanic, not a JCDEC power.

But Louisville's Mayor and Metro Council moved from **partisan** to **nonpartisan** elections starting 2026 per HB 388 (2024):
- See `jcdec-party-funding-laws.md` companion corpus for the HB 388 + KRS 67C statutory citation
- Nonpartisan races are NOT a "Democratic Primary Election" within the meaning of `[KDP Art. I.I.a]`
- Therefore, the prohibition does not apply
- The party can endorse a candidate in a nonpartisan race

LDP endorsements thus exist in a narrow window: only for Louisville Mayor and Metro Council (nonpartisan as of 2026), running on a primary-then-general top-2 format per Louisville's election code. See `reference_louisville_nonpartisan_top2.md` in memory.

## Authority — where the policy lives

The endorsement process **is NOT in the LJCDP bylaws**. `[LJCDP §26]` lists Standing Committees and does not include Endorsement Process. Per portal CLAUDE.md (April 2026):

> "Endorsement votes — Not in bylaws directly — the 60% threshold, secret ballot via ElectionRunner, and no-proxies rule for endorsement votes live in Endorsement Process Committee policy, not the bylaws themselves."

JCDEC currently runs:
- **8 standing committees:** Bylaws, Candidate Recruitment, Communications, Events, Facilities, Finance, Training, Volunteer
- **3 ad hoc committees:** Branding, **Endorsement Process**, Platform

Endorsement Process being ad hoc means its rules sit in committee policy adopted by the CEC or by the committee with CEC ratification, **not in §26**. They persist until rescinded or until §26 is amended to make it standing.

## The core mechanics

Per portal CLAUDE.md and LDP operating practice:

### 60% threshold

Endorsement requires **60%** of votes cast — not a simple majority. This is the LDP-specific bar, higher than standard CEC majority votes.

This applies to **endorsement votes only**. Other CEC business (motions, removals, vacancy filling, etc.) uses LJCDP-specified thresholds — usually majority `[LJCDP §15.1]`, `[LJCDP §16.1]`, `[LJCDP §21.1]` or 2/3 for same-meeting new business `[LJCDP §25.2]`.

### Secret ballot via ElectionRunner

Endorsement votes are conducted by **secret ballot using the ElectionRunner platform**. This is a deliberate procedural choice:
- Allows accurate vote tallies for the 60% calculation
- Removes social pressure from in-person hand-raising / voice votes
- Creates an auditable record
- Permits the vote to span a defined window (typically a meeting evening), not just a single show-of-hands moment

ElectionRunner is the adopted tool; its use is operational practice, not a bylaw requirement. If the committee or CEC adopted a different platform in the future, the secret-ballot requirement is the rule of substance.

### No proxies during endorsement votes

Endorsement votes are treated as a special category — **no proxies** are accepted. This is operationally consistent with `[LJCDP §23.1]` and `[KDP Art. III.A.a]`, which both prohibit proxies for "the conduct of an election or dismissal of any party officer." An endorsement is functionally an election event; the no-proxies rule applies by analogy and by adopted committee policy.

**Operationally:** for endorsement-vote evenings, only members present in person (or, depending on adopted procedure, present via Zoom in real-time) can vote. The bylaws don't directly govern this — it's adopted Endorsement Process Committee policy + meeting practice.

### Quorum requirement

Quorum for the CEC meeting at which endorsement is taken follows `[LJCDP §22.1]` — 40% of LD Chairs + LD VCs + LD At-Large members, in person or by proxy. But because no proxies are accepted for the endorsement vote itself, and because of `[LJCDP §22.1.1]` ("Proxies can not be counted to establish a quorum to conduct business of an election or dismissal of any Party officer"), the endorsement-vote quorum is effectively **40% in person** for purposes of the vote.

In practice, endorsement meetings are well-attended and quorum is rarely the binding constraint.

## Timeline (typical year — January–February)

The Endorsement Process Committee operates a defined timeline for any nonpartisan race endorsement, typically running in January and February of an election year:

1. **Candidate invitation** — Committee invites all filed nonpartisan Democratic candidates for Mayor and Metro Council to participate in the endorsement process. Filing deadlines are set by the Jefferson County Clerk under KRS Title X.
2. **Candidate questionnaire** — Standardized questionnaire goes to candidates. Responses are recorded.
3. **Candidate interview / forum** — Committee interviews candidates or hosts a forum. Format adopted by committee.
4. **Committee recommendation** — Endorsement Process Committee deliberates and recommends a candidate (or recommends "no endorsement") to the full CEC.
5. **Full CEC endorsement vote** — At a regular or special CEC meeting, the question of endorsement goes to a secret-ballot ElectionRunner vote. **60% threshold to endorse**. No proxies.
6. **Public announcement** — If endorsement passes, the party publicizes the endorsement and provides general-election-level support (resources, mailers, social, ground game, etc.).

The exact timeline + format is adopted by the Endorsement Process Committee with CEC ratification where required. Confirm with Endorsement Process Chair for any given cycle.

## What endorsement does NOT cover

### Partisan primaries

Endorsement is **flatly prohibited** by `[KDP Art. I.I.a]`. JCDEC cannot endorse a Democrat over another Democrat in any partisan primary — federal, state, county, judicial, magisterial, school board (where partisan), etc.

The Mayor and Metro Council races are nonpartisan as of 2026 per HB 388. Before HB 388, the Democratic Primary winner was the Democratic nominee for the General Election in those races. Now there is no Democratic Primary for those offices — there's a nonpartisan top-2 primary and a nonpartisan general. The party can endorse in either round.

### Judicial races

KY judicial races are **always nonpartisan** under KY Const. § 117 (Circuit Court, District Court, Court of Appeals, KY Supreme Court). JCDEC does not endorse in judicial races by KY judicial conduct convention — there's a separate set of considerations under Canon 4 of the KY Code of Judicial Conduct (SCR 4.300). See `jcdec-party-funding-laws.md` companion corpus.

The endorsement process described here is for **Louisville Mayor + Metro Council only**, the two HB 388 nonpartisan offices.

### JCPS Board of Education, Soil & Water Conservation

These are nonpartisan but historically have not been part of the LDP endorsement process. Could be added by CEC vote; not currently in scope.

## Mayor and Metro Council nonpartisan format (Louisville)

Mayor + Metro Council races are **TOP-2 ADVANCE** regardless of primary share. **A candidate getting >50% in the nonpartisan primary does NOT win outright** — the top-2 advance to a contested General Election.

This is critical for endorsement strategy and General-Election plans. From memory file `reference_louisville_nonpartisan_top2.md`:

> "2026 Mayor: Greenberg (52.5%) vs Parrish-Wright (26.3%) is a contested General, not a Greenberg outright win."

For the General Election round, the LDP endorsement (if granted in the primary round) typically carries forward, but the committee may revisit if circumstances change.

## When LDP endorses, what does that do?

A formal LDP endorsement (60% threshold passed) means:
1. The endorsement is publicly announced under LDP brand
2. The party may use Party Building / Party Operations funds (county committee category — see `jcdec-party-funding-laws.md`) on the endorsed candidate's behalf consistent with KRS Chapter 121 limits
3. The candidate may use the LDP endorsement in their campaign materials
4. The endorsement creates a coordination expectation with the LDP volunteer pipeline + canvass operations

What endorsement does NOT do:
- It does not bind individual CEC members to support the candidate personally (per the equivalent of `[KDP Art. I.I.c]` for nonpartisan endorsements)
- It does not transfer to the General Election automatically — the CEC may revisit
- It does not change the candidate's filing status with the Jefferson County Clerk

## "Why 60%?"

The 60% threshold is higher than a standard majority because:
- It reflects a meaningful consensus, not a bare plurality of attendance
- It guards against factional capture of an endorsement
- It's the historic LDP threshold and is the bar Endorsement Process Committee operates against

The 60% threshold is not anchored in any bylaw — it's adopted Endorsement Process Committee policy ratified by the CEC. If a future committee or CEC vote changed it (e.g., to 2/3 or to simple majority), the change would persist as the new operating threshold.

## What the committee owns vs what the CEC owns

| Responsibility | Owner |
|---|---|
| Candidate invitation + questionnaire + interview | Endorsement Process Committee |
| Recommendation to full CEC | Endorsement Process Committee |
| Final vote on endorsement | Full CEC, by secret ballot ElectionRunner, 60% threshold |
| Public announcement | LDP Chair / Communications Committee, after CEC vote |
| Ongoing coordination with endorsed candidate | LDP Chair + Volunteer Committee + Communications Committee |
